Areal Precipitation Optimisation for Rainfall-Runoff Modelling

Technical University of Munich Grade: 1.0 (Excellent)

Abstract

Accurate areal precipitation estimation is a critical input for rainfall-runoff modelling, particularly in complex terrain where orographic effects introduce significant spatial variability. This study addresses systematic biases in standard interpolation methods by developing a directional DEM smoothing framework combined with stratified kriging.

The approach modifies digital elevation model (DEM) data to better reflect the precipitation-generating mechanisms of prevailing wind directions, producing a topographic predictor that more accurately captures orographic enhancement. Stratified kriging is then applied using this modified DEM as a covariate, improving spatial interpolation of rain gauge observations.

Applied to a study catchment in the Alpine region, the method achieved a 45.4% reduction in areal precipitation estimation error compared to conventional kriging with the original DEM. The results demonstrate that targeted pre-processing of topographic inputs can substantially improve the quality of spatially distributed precipitation fields used in hydrological modelling.

Flood Risk Reduction: Micro-Level Assessment in Roßhaupten

Technical University of Munich Roßhaupten, Bavaria, Germany

Abstract

This project presents a micro-level flood risk assessment for the municipality of Roßhaupten, Bavaria, integrating high-resolution geospatial data with hydraulic modelling to evaluate current flood exposure and identify risk reduction measures.

LiDAR-derived digital terrain models were used to characterise the local drainage network and identify hydraulic bottlenecks. Flood inundation extents were simulated under multiple return period scenarios using HEC-RAS, and results were intersected with land use and infrastructure datasets to quantify exposure at the parcel level.

The study evaluated culvert design alternatives and proposed targeted infrastructure interventions to reduce peak flow velocities and inundation depths in residential areas. Findings provide a basis for cost-effective, locally adapted flood risk management strategies for small Alpine communities.

Radiation-Enhanced Snowmelt Runoff Modelling — Inn River Basin

Technical University of Munich Inn River Basin, Alps

Abstract

Snowmelt is a dominant driver of spring and early summer runoff in Alpine catchments. Standard temperature-index models often fail to capture the spatial heterogeneity of melt rates across complex terrain, where aspect and slope strongly influence incoming solar radiation.

This study enhanced a snowmelt runoff model for the Inn River basin by incorporating spatially distributed shortwave radiation data derived from Google Earth Engine. Terrain-corrected radiation grids were computed from digital elevation models and integrated as an energy input term alongside temperature data, replacing the spatially uniform radiation assumption of the baseline model.

The radiation-enhanced model demonstrated improved agreement with observed discharge hydrographs, particularly during the onset of the melt season, when spatial variability in energy inputs is most pronounced. The approach offers a computationally efficient pathway to improve snowmelt model performance without requiring full energy-balance parameterisation.

Glacier Dynamics in the Marshyangdi Basin, Nepal — A 30-Year Analysis

Kathmandu University / TUM Marshyangdi Basin, Nepal

Abstract

Himalayan glaciers are among the most sensitive indicators of climate change, and their retreat has significant implications for water security across South Asia. This study investigates three decades of glacier dynamics in the Marshyangdi River basin, Nepal, using multi-temporal satellite imagery and machine learning classification.

Sentinel-2 and Landsat imagery spanning 1990–2020 were processed in Google Earth Engine to generate annual glacier extent maps. A supervised machine learning classifier was trained on spectral and topographic features to distinguish glaciated from non-glaciated surfaces, with particular attention to debris-covered glacier identification — a persistent challenge in Himalayan remote sensing.

Results reveal a statistically significant reduction in total glaciated area over the study period, with accelerating retreat rates observed in the most recent decade. The findings contribute to the evidence base for climate-driven cryosphere change in the Hindu Kush Himalaya region and have implications for downstream hydropower and water resource planning.

Post-Disaster Land Use Change Analysis — 2015 Nepal Earthquake

Kathmandu University Nepal (Gorkha Earthquake Affected Districts)

Abstract

The April 2015 Gorkha earthquake (Mw 7.8) caused widespread landscape disruption across central Nepal, triggering thousands of landslides and altering land cover patterns across a large area. This study quantifies post-disaster land use and land cover (LULC) change using multi-temporal satellite imagery and supervised classification.

Pre- and post-earthquake Landsat imagery was classified using a maximum likelihood classifier to map six LULC categories across the most severely affected districts. Change detection analysis identified areas of significant transition, particularly the conversion of vegetated slopes to bare land and debris deposits.

The study documents both the immediate landscape impacts of the earthquake and early recovery trajectories over a three-year monitoring period. Results highlight the utility of freely available satellite data for rapid post-disaster environmental assessment and can inform land use planning and ecological restoration efforts in disaster-affected regions.
